Learning benefits associated with synaesthesia

Research suggests that children with synesthesia may show the following skills and abilities. These differences were found on average, rather than in every child with synesthesia tested.

Processing speed: Children with synesthesia in one study (ref 1) were quicker than other children at finding specific objects hidden in a picture (they were looking for animals hidden among many other objects). This study suggests that, on average, children with synesthesia can process information faster than other people.  

Memory skills: It is now well-established that synesthetes can better remember information, both over the short and long term (ref 2). They particularly excel in memory for language information, such as a list of words.

Literacy and Numeracy skills: Children with synethesia have a wider vocabulary than their peers, and are more confident about their reading skills (ref 3). Children with synesthesia also have a better intuitive number sense. This is the ability to estimate quantity without needing to physically count. Imagine looking at a field of sheep and cows – most people could intuitively know if there were more cows or sheep even without counting. Children with synesthesia are  particularly good at this (ref 4). 

Creativity:   Adult synaesthetes have been found to show a greater interest in creative arts and are more likely to take up a creative occupation (eg artistic, musician) than non-synaesthetes (refs 5-7).  Children and adults with synesthesia have been found to be higher than other people in the trait ‘Openness to experience’:  a personality trait linked to aesthetic interests, active imagination and intellectual curiosity (refs 8-10).
