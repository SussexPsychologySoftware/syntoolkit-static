Grapheme colour synaesthesia case study

.leading to avoidance of school work

---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

What is grapheme-colour synaesthesia? Grapheme-colour synaesthesia is experiencing colours when hearing, reading, saying, or thinking about letters or numbers. So for example, the letter A may feel intrinsically red, 4 may feel blue, and so on. Grapheme-colour synaesthesia derives from subtle differences in the structure and function of synaesthetes brains (ref 2) and is found in approximately 1-2% of the population (ref 1). People with grapheme-colour syneasthesia can experience it in two distinct ways. The first group are called projector synaesthetes (ref 3) because they experience colours projected into space, as if they were part of the real-world (below left). Sometimes this projection of colour can overlay onto the printed letters of a written page. The second group of synaesthetes are called associators (below right), and this is more common. For these synaesthetes, the colours of letters and numbers are seen in the minds eye, or instead, the synaesthete might simply know what the colours must be. This is true even if the synaesthete encounter letters or numbers in a different colour. Its comparable to knowing the colour of a lemon, even if you see a picture of it in black and white. The difference between projectors and associators can also be seen in scans from brain imaging; while both have more connections in the brain than people without synaesthesia, projectors have even more than associators (ref 4).

Not all synaesthetes have colours for all letters and numbers, and children have fewer fixed colours than adults (ref 5). In addition, it is common to have colours for numbers but not letters, or vice versa. The colours for letters and numbers often differ from one synaesthetes to the next (e.g., one synaesthete might think A is red, while another thinks A is blue), although there are some notable commonalities across synaesthetes. For example, the letter O is white for over half of synaesthetes whilst A is commonly red (ref 6). It is sometimes thought that synaesthesia must be learned from early experiences such as wooden blocks or fridge magnets, but this only accounts for a relatively small proportion of synaesthetes (ref 7). The majority of people with synaesthesia are unaware of its roots, and have no recollection of inventing their associations in any way whatsoever.

Case studies: Male, aged 8. The child had coloured letters and numbers causing avoidance of school work. Further investigation revealed that the child found the colours of certain numbers unpleasant and overwhelming, meaning he would avoid writing them at school. A second case is a female aged 12 with grapheme-colour synaesthesia and a high academic achiever. The child showed an atypical aversion to certain areas of mathematics, rising to the extent of a mild phobia. Further investigation revealed that her synaesthetically coloured numbers were problematic, for example in fractions where coloured numerators and denominators were non-logical when it came to resolving problems (e.g., the colours of did not match those of 0.75).
