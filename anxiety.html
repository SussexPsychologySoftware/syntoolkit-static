 Anxiety and synaesthesia

 

Grapheme-colour synaesthesia is significantly comorbid with anxiety disorder. In other words, both adults and children with synaesthesia have higher rates of anxiety than would otherwise be expected. Carmichael et al. (ref 23) tested 3,742 people (95 synaesthetes and 3,647 controls) and found that the synaesthetes reported diagnoses of anxiety disorder significantly more often than controls. The same authors replicated their findings on new population of 120 synaesthetes and 166 non-synaesthetes. They found that people who had been diagnosed with anxiety were 1.9 times more likely to be synaesthetes than non-synaesthetes. A third study produced a similar finding in children: 30% of children with grapheme-colour synaesthesia had symptoms suggestive of Anxiety Disorder, which was two and a half times higher than non-synaesthetes. Recently, Ward (ref 2) evaluated studies that have found elevated levels of Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) in individuals with synaesthesia. The author suggested that high levels of visual imagery and more intense autobiographical memories may predispose synaesthetes to the flashbacks experienced in PTSD.

 

Anxiety disorder is among the most problematic mental health disorders in childhood. Childhood anxiety also brings a three-fold increase for experiencing depression or anxiety in later life. Recognising synaesthesia means recognising risk factors for anxiety, where early intervention can significantly improve life-outcomes. Early diagnosis of synaesthesia can therefore provide an opportunity to conduct risk assessments for anxiety (eg. are they missing school? disengaged in class?). This can indicate whether specialist input is needed. 

 

References

1. Carmichael, et al. (2019). Is there a burden attached to synaesthesia? Health screening of synaesthetes in the general population. British Journal of Psychology, 110. 

2. Ward (2021). Is synaesthesia a predisposing factor to post-traumatic stress disorder? Frontiers in Bioscience-Scholar, 13.
