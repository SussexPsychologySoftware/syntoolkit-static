Educational and well-being issues: mild negative impacts 

Synaesthesia sometimes has negative outcomes. Mild negative reports include (a) visual distractions (eg. sounds cause colours that block child's view of the whiteboard), (b) visual confusions (eg. child confuses numbers and colours when doing sums or other calculations), (c) difficulty in classroom environment (eg. alphabet posters show 'wrong' colours for him/her, which can cause synaesthetes discomfort), (d) upset for children if they are disbelieved by teachers and school-mates. Synaesthesia may be completely unknown to their teachers even though it is impacting progress at school. As well as these first-hand report, studies have shown synaesthetes can have specific impairments. For example, grapheme-colour synaesthetes are good at perceiving colours, but are poorer at perceiving movement (ref 1). And their numerical skills are good for grapheme-colour synaesthetes (who quickly estimate the number of objects in a group faster than other people; ref 2), but poorer for sequence-space synaesthetes (who are worse at mental multiplication than other people; ref 3). 

If the child experiences some negative outcomes related to their synaesthesia, you may wish to liaise with school health professionals (eg. SEND-CO) so that everyone can stay informed about how synaesthesia may be affecting the child. With parental permission, scores can be shared with the SEND-CO or equivalent (i.e. school support officer for children with learning differences). And parents can complete our printable information sheet (www.syntoolkit.org\parent) to describe their child’s experiences and share with teachers. When everyone understands exactly how synaesthesia impacts the child’s schooling or well-being, it will be easier to implement specific management strategies to help the child cope more easily.

References

1. Banissy, et al. (2013). Synesthesia for color is linked to improved color perception but reduced motion perception. Psychological science, 24.

2. Rinaldi, et al. (2020). Numeracy skills in child synaesthetes: Evidence from grapheme-colour synaesthesia. Cortex, 126.

3. Ward, et al. (2009). The impact of visuo-spatial number forms on simple arithmetic. cortex, 45.
