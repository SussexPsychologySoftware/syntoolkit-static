Lexical-gustatory synaesthesia case study

…..causing distracting/unpleasant tastes sensations

----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------What is lexical-gustatory synaesthesia? Lexical-gustatory synaesthesia is experiencing tastes and flavours when hearing, reading, saying, or thinking about words. The prevalence is unknown but is somewhere less than 0.2% of the population and may be rarer still (ref 1). Common triggers are often the names of people and places. One famous lexical-gustatory synaesthete, James Wannerton, has created a map of the London underground system showing the tastes he experiences from the names of different stations (e.g., Kings Cross station floods his mouth with the taste of fruit cake). Tastes are often related to foods the synaesthete ate as a child, therefore some tastes (e.g., chocolate) are more common than others (e.g., alcohol) (ref 2). But not all synaesthetic flavours are foods and are not all are pleasant; James Wannerton tastes burnt matches when he hears the station name Holborn. Some lexical-gustatory synaesthetes feel “tastes in the mouth” (i.e., they taste, as if there were real food-flavourings dropped in the mouth) while others feel ‘tastes in the mind’ (i.e., they know what the taste of any word must be). These are called projector and associator synaesthesia, respectively. Taste associations often seem random, but sometimes share sounds in common with the trigger-word. For example, the word bandana might trigger the taste of banana, the name Tony might taste of macaroni, and the name Leonard might taste of lemons (ref 2). Lexical-gustatory synesthesia has been traced to small differences in the taste cortex of synesthetes’ brains (ref 3).

Case studies. Female, aged 7 with no formal diagnoses but with lexical-gustatory synaesthesia, as well as letter-personification synaesthesia. Each day of the week filled her mouth with a specific flavour (e.g., Monday was mashed potato). Relatedly, certain emotions triggered flavours in the mouth, particularly negative emotions which flooded her mouth with a strong taste of gingerbread. This caused her to feel nausea alongside the negative emotion itself, which the child found distressing. The second case is a female, aged 8 years, with a diagnosis of high functioning autism. The child reported “choking on hair” while her mouth was empty. We traced this back to lexical-gustatory synaesthesia, where hearing words fills the mouth with sensations of flavour or texture; in this case a texture resembling hair when she heard certain words. Although our SYNTOOLKIT does not test for this variant of synaesthesia in children, it could nonetheless identify a more generalised synaesthetic phenotype, because this child also had coloured letters and numbers. This is because multiple variants of synaesthesia tend to co-occur within the same individual (ref 4). In other words, children with lexical-gustatory synaesthesia are also likely to have one of the common variants targeted in our test. Hence, by focussing on two very common forms of synaesthesia, our test can identify synaesthetes even if the initial presentation is different.
